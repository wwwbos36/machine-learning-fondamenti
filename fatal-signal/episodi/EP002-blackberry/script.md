# EP002 — BlackBerry — Script

## [HOOK] (0–60s)

[B-ROLL: INTRO-001 — dark corporate hallway dolly-in, navy+amber]

At its peak, this phone was in the hands of presidents, CEOs, and movie stars. Around half of all smartphones sold in the United States carried its name. [VERIFICARE %: ~50% nel 2009]

[B-ROLL: 2000s footage of people thumb-typing on physical keyboards — STOCK/ARCHIVIO]

People didn't just use it. They were addicted to it. The world even had a nickname for that addiction: "CrackBerry."

[B-ROLL: close-up of a physical QWERTY keyboard clicking — STOCK/ARCHIVIO]

That little keyboard, with its satisfying click, made millions of people feel powerful and productive.

[B-ROLL: TENS-001 — cracked office window, dark city]

And then, in just a few years, that very same keyboard — the thing that made BlackBerry indispensable — was the thing that buried it.

[B-ROLL: FALL-001 — empty office]

BlackBerry didn't disappear because it ran out of money or ideas. It disappeared because it was certain. Certain that it already knew what a phone was supposed to be.

This is the story of how being right for so long made a company unable to see when it had become wrong.

## [ORIGINI] (60s–3min)

[B-ROLL: RISE-001 reversed mood — 1980s Canadian tech office, archival — STOCK/ARCHIVIO]

The company began in 1984, in Waterloo, Canada, under the name Research In Motion — founded by an engineering student named Mike Lazaridis and his childhood friend Douglas Fregin.

[B-ROLL: TRANS-001 — circuit board pull-back]

A few years later, a businessman named Jim Balsillie joined and became co-CEO alongside Lazaridis — an unusual structure of two men sharing the top job that would matter enormously later.

[B-ROLL: early BlackBerry email device — STOCK/ARCHIVIO]

Their breakthrough idea was simple but powerful: secure email, anywhere, on a device with a real keyboard. Not for teenagers. For people who worked.

[B-ROLL: corporate office, suits checking devices — STOCK/ARCHIVIO]

And that focus was everything. BlackBerry wasn't trying to be fun. It was trying to be serious — reliable, secure, professional. For a while, that was exactly what the most important people in the world wanted.

## [ASCESA] (3–6min)

[B-ROLL: RISE-001 — glowing campus at dawn, crane up]

Through the 2000s, BlackBerry became the standard for business and government. [GRAFICO: US smartphone share, BlackBerry peak]

[B-ROLL: trading floors, government offices, people on BlackBerrys — STOCK/ARCHIVIO]

If you had a BlackBerry, you mattered. It was a status symbol that said you were too busy and too important to be unreachable.

[B-ROLL: APPS-001 style — messages flowing between devices — HIGGSFIELD concettuale]

Its messaging service, BBM, created a private network that locked users in. At its height, around September 2012, BlackBerry had roughly eighty million subscribers worldwide. ✅

[GRAFICO: BlackBerry subscribers climbing]

[B-ROLL: TENS-001 subtle — foreshadowing]

BlackBerry had built something rare: not just a product, but a habit. The keyboard, the click, the blinking red light of a new message. It owned the way an entire generation of professionals communicated. And owning something that completely is exactly when a company stops asking whether it might be wrong.

## [ERRORE FATALE] (6–9min)

[B-ROLL: DEVICE-001 — sleek glass phone rotating on dark surface — HIGGSFIELD concettuale]

In January 2007, Apple announced the iPhone. No physical keyboard. Just a sheet of glass. ✅

[B-ROLL: finger typing on a flat touchscreen — STOCK/ARCHIVIO]

BlackBerry's leaders weren't impressed. Where were the buttons? How could you type on glass? Where was the security, the battery life, the seriousness? To them, the iPhone looked like a toy. A consumer gadget that real professionals would never tolerate.

[B-ROLL: macro of a device being opened on a workbench — HIGGSFIELD concettuale]

And yet — privately, Mike Lazaridis understood the danger better than most. When he took an early iPhone apart, he reportedly said, "They've put a Mac in this thing." [VERIFICARE citazione: "Losing the Signal"] He saw that Apple hadn't built a better phone. It had built a small computer. The problem wasn't that BlackBerry couldn't see it. The problem was what they did next.

[B-ROLL: FEAR-001 style — executives dismissing something in a dark boardroom — HIGGSFIELD concettuale]

And here is the trap. They weren't wrong about their own past. The keyboard and the security really had made BlackBerry great.

**The fatal flaw was mistaking what had made them great for what would keep them great.** They defended the keyboard not as a feature, but as an identity. And an identity is very hard to let go of.

[B-ROLL: PATHS-001 — two diverging glowing paths — HIGGSFIELD concettuale]

There was a deeper blind spot too. BlackBerry sold to IT managers — the people who bought phones for companies. The iPhone sold to employees. And employees fell in love with it, then walked into work and demanded to use it.

The phone didn't have to beat BlackBerry in the boardroom. It beat it in people's pockets first, and the boardroom followed.

## [CADUTA] (9–12min)

[B-ROLL: device with cracked screen on dark surface — HIGGSFIELD concettuale]

When BlackBerry finally reacted, it reacted in a panic. It rushed out a touchscreen phone, the Storm, which disappointed almost everyone. [VERIFICARE: Storm 2008]

[B-ROLL: abandoned tablet on a desk — STOCK/ARCHIVIO]

In April 2011, it launched a tablet, the PlayBook, that arrived unfinished — it shipped without even native email — and flopped, leading to a write-down of around half a billion dollars. ✅

[B-ROLL: FALL-001 — empty open-plan office, melancholic]

Meanwhile, the two co-CEOs struggled to steer fast enough. In January 2012, both Lazaridis and Balsillie finally stepped down. And by the time BlackBerry launched a truly modern operating system, BlackBerry 10, it was January 2013 — years too late, into a world already divided between Apple and Android. ✅

[GRAFICO: market share and value collapsing]

Subscribers left. The value collapsed. And in September 2016, BlackBerry stopped designing its own phones, soon licensing its name to another company, TCL. ✅

The phone of the powerful had become a phone almost no one wanted to be seen with.

## [OGGI] (12–14min)

[B-ROLL: modern car dashboard, software interfaces — STOCK/ARCHIVIO]

So where is BlackBerry now?

It still exists — but it left your pocket completely. Today, BlackBerry is a software and cybersecurity company. Through its QNX division, its software quietly runs inside more than 275 million vehicles on the road — far from the phones it once ruled. ✅

[B-ROLL: Apple and Android phones in many hands — STOCK/ARCHIVIO]

The winners were the companies that understood the phone had become something new — personal, visual, fun, and built around apps. Apple and Android took the consumer first, and then took the businesses too.

## [RIFLESSIONE] (14–16min)

[B-ROLL: THESIS-001 — falling chess king, slow motion]

BlackBerry's story is not about a company that couldn't build. It built brilliantly — for the world as it used to be.

[B-ROLL: BULB-001 — single flickering bulb in a dark room — HIGGSFIELD concettuale]

It lost because it treated its greatest strength as a permanent truth. The keyboard, the security, the seriousness — these were choices that fit a moment. BlackBerry turned them into an identity, and then defended that identity long after the world had moved on.

[B-ROLL: INTRO-001 reprise — dark hallway fading to black]

That's the quiet danger of success. The very things that make you win teach you to stop questioning. And the more certain you are, the less you can see.

The fatal flaw was mistaking what made them great for what would keep them great — and defending the keyboard like it was the future.
